MEDUSA

Hesiod tells in the Theogony that the beautiful Medusa, daughter of Ceto and Phorcys, fell in love with Poseidon and joined him in the temple of Athena. But according to Ovid in The Metamorphoses, Medusa was raped by the implacable god of the sea. Be that as it may, Athena, enraged by the sexual desecration of her temple, punished Medusa: she transformed her beautiful hair into a nest of snakes and cursed her beautiful eyes so that no one could look at them again or she would end up turned into stone. And since the Greek gods liked to play with the lives of mortals, Athena and Hermes guided Perseus, the hero of the day, to behead the cruel? Gorgon. That is the popular version of a myth of pre-Hellenic origins. Because before classical Greece Medusa appeared as queen of Libya, an incarnation of sexuality and mother Earth. We had to wait many centuries for the myth of the Greek Medusa, of the monstrous woman punished for her femininity, of the hetaira scorned, to be reread.
